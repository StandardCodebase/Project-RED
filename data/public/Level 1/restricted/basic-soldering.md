Soldering is the process of joining electronic components to a PCB using a metal alloy (solder) melted by a hot iron. A good solder joint is shiny, cone-shaped, and adheres firmly to both the component lead and the pad.

## Equipment

- **Soldering iron**: 25–60W with temperature control. Set to 320–370°C for most work.
- **Solder**: 60/40 or 63/37 tin/lead rosin-core, 0.6–0.8mm diameter. Lead-free works but requires higher temps.
- **Flux**: Helps solder flow and prevents oxidation. Most rosin-core solder has enough built in.
- **Brass wire tip cleaner**: Preferred over wet sponge — keeps tip temperature stable.
- **Helping hands or PCB holder**: Keeps the board still while you work.

## The joint sequence

1. Tin the iron tip — melt a small amount of solder onto the clean tip before starting.
2. Place the component lead through the PCB hole and bend it slightly to hold it in place.
3. Touch the iron to the junction of the lead and pad simultaneously — heat both, not just one.
4. After 1–2 seconds, feed solder into the junction (not onto the iron).
5. Remove the solder, then the iron. Do not move the joint for 3–5 seconds while it solidifies.
6. Clip the excess lead flush with the joint.

## Common problems

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Cold joint (dull, grainy) | Not enough heat or moved while cooling | Reheat and add fresh solder |
| Solder bridge | Too much solder between adjacent pads | Wick away with desoldering braid |
| Lifted pad | Iron held too long | Replace PCB or use wire jumper |
| Solder balls | Contaminated flux or too much solder | Clean board with isopropyl alcohol |

## Safety

Work in a ventilated space. Flux fumes are irritating. Wash hands after handling lead solder. The iron tip reaches 350°C — treat it accordingly.
